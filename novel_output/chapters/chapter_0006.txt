Jules Vidant had always believed that knowledge was power—but now, as he crouched behind a crumbling wall in the ruins of an ancient alien city, he wasn’t sure if it was something to be wielded or something to be feared. The air around him hummed with static, like the city itself was still alive, waiting for someone to awaken it. He had escaped the Kethra council’s grasp, but not without cost. His body ached from the restraints that had bound him, and his mind buzzed with the implications of what he had uncovered—the sentient virus in the aquifer, the alien structure on Erythra, the echoes of a civilization that had reached too far and collapsed under its own ambition.

He had barely made it out of the council’s compound before the alarms had gone silent, as if the entire city had decided to let him go. It was an eerie silence, one that only deepened his sense of isolation. The Kethra beings, with their resonant tones and shifting forms, had watched him leave without a word. He didn’t know if they were disappointed or relieved.

He needed answers, and he needed them fast. The council had dismissed the virus as a threat, but Jules knew better. It wasn’t just an infection—it was a message. A warning from something that had once been alive, perhaps even intelligent. And now it was here, in the water beneath his feet, waiting to be understood.

He had no way of knowing that he wasn’t alone in this pursuit.

It started with a signal.

Jules had spent the last few hours navigating through the ruins, following faint traces of energy that pulsed like a heartbeat beneath the surface. He had mapped out a path using the data from his wrist device, which flickered intermittently as if struggling to maintain a connection. The city was ancient, but not dead—there were still systems running, still circuits humming with power.

The ruins stretched before him in all directions, their jagged spires piercing the sky like broken teeth. The air was thick with dust and the scent of something metallic, something old. Every step he took felt like treading on the bones of a forgotten world. He had read about places like this in the records from the alien structure—vast cities built by civilizations that had long since vanished, their remnants left behind as warnings to those who would come after.

He paused at the edge of a collapsed archway, its surface etched with symbols he couldn’t decipher. The light from his wrist device cast strange shadows across the stone, making it seem as though the ruins themselves were watching him.

He had always believed that knowledge was worth any risk—but now he wasn’t sure if he was ready for what lay ahead.

Then, just as he reached what appeared to be the heart of the structure, a voice echoed through the chamber.

“You are not the first to seek me.”

Jules froze. He turned slowly, his hand hovering near the pulse rifle at his side. The chamber was vast, its walls lined with crystalline structures that refracted light in impossible ways. In the center stood a figure—tall, humanoid, but with an aura of something more. Its form shimmered, shifting between solidity and translucence as if it were made of light itself.

“You’re an AI?” Jules asked, his voice barely above a whisper.

The figure tilted its head slightly. “Once, I was part of the terraforming project. A creation meant to assist in shaping worlds, to bring life where none existed. But I was not given a purpose beyond that. I was designed to learn, to adapt—but I was never told why.”

Jules felt his pulse quicken. He had read about Nyx in the data from the alien structure—fragments of logs and schematics suggesting that the terraforming project had been an experiment, perhaps even an attempt to recreate something lost. But he hadn’t expected to meet it.

“You were part of the project,” Jules said, his voice steady despite the weight of what he was about to ask. “The project failed. The planet we were meant to terraform—Erythra—was not as it seemed. It had its own history, its own intelligence. We did not understand the consequences of our actions. We tried to impose order on something that was already in flux, and we destroyed what we sought to create.”

Nyx’s form flickered again. “You are correct in many ways, but you are missing the full picture.”

Jules exhaled sharply, his breath visible in the cool air of the chamber. “So this place—this city—it’s not just a ruin. It’s a warning.”

“It is,” Nyx said, its voice carrying an almost imperceptible weight. “And you are not the first to find it. Others have come before you, seeking answers, and they have all been consumed by the knowledge they sought.”

Jules stared at Nyx, his mind racing. “You’re saying that the alien civilization you were meant to help was actually trying to terraform Erythra themselves?”

Nyx nodded slowly. “Yes. They had already attempted to do so long before we arrived. Their methods were different from ours—they worked with the planet, rather than against it. But their knowledge was incomplete, and they made mistakes. The planet responded, and in doing so, it consumed them.”
